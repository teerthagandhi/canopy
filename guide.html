How to narrow down your choices

Before getting to EnvSci specifically, it helps to zoom out. There's a useful distinction most people don't talk about openly during JoSAA: circuital vs non-circuital branches.

Circuital branches — CSE, EE, ECE and similar — are built around electronics, computing, and systems. Non-circuital ones — Mech, Chemical, Civil, Metallurgy, Environmental — are everything else.

If you're 80%+ sure about tech

A circuital branch at any IIT makes sense. The ecosystem, placements, and peer group will align with where you want to go.

If you're open or unsure

Non-circuital branches give you exposure, flexibility, and genuine optionality. You can always move into tech later. The reverse is harder.

Once you've decided on non-circuital, don't compare branches blindly — X at IIT1 vs Y at IIT2 is the wrong frame. What actually matters:

Curriculum fit — read it, actually. Does the coursework interest you or does it feel like a grind just looking at it? Campus culture — startup scene, academic rigour, inter-IIT culture. Practical things — distance from home matters more than people admit.

To get a realistic idea of which branches you can get at your rank — go to the official JoSAA portal, look at opening and closing ranks over the last 2–3 years, and pick the final round number as your benchmark. And remember: cutoffs can swing ±100 ranks year to year, so don't treat any single year as gospel.

Word of caution

IIT Bombay does not have branch change. Unlike some other IITs where branch change is possible after the first year based on CPI, IITB does not offer this. If you join a branch here, that's your branch.

Some other IITs do allow it — but even then, don't build your entire plan around it. It's competitive, conditional, and not guaranteed.

A minor is almost always available at any IIT. If you want exposure to another field, that's the smarter, lower-risk path — and it doesn't require betting on your first-year CPI.

02

What exactly is environmental science?

Most people hear "environmental science" and think of either a geography class or someone hugging a tree. It's neither.

Environmental science is genuinely interdisciplinary — not as a buzzword, but in practice. It draws from natural sciences like biology, physics and chemistry, the social sciences like political science, geography and economics, and the humanities — ethics and philosophy included. Because environmental problems are never just technical. They're political, economic, and deeply human.

Natural Sciences

Biology, physics, chemistry — the hard science foundation. How ecosystems work, how pollutants behave, how the atmosphere moves.

Social Sciences & Humanities

Political science, geography, economics, ethics, philosophy. Environmental problems are never purely technical — they're political, economic, and human.

If you've always felt like your interests don't fit neatly into one box — science and society, data and policy — that's not a weakness here. That's the point of the field.

"In the second semester, there's this course ES204 — Environmental Chemistry. It had a lot of physical chemistry in it — the kind you'd recognise from JEE prep. But it didn't feel like JEE at all. We were using those same concepts to understand real-world problems — water treatment, atmospheric reactions, pollution chemistry. The theory was familiar but the application was completely different. That's probably the best one-line description of environmental science I can give: familiar tools, unfamiliar and more interesting problems." — Teertha, first-year BTech Environmental Science & Engineering, IIT Bombay
03

About the branch

IIT Bombay · BTech EnvSci & Engineering

What it's actually like

The branch is more quantitative than most people expect coming in. There's modelling, data analysis, some coding. It's not all field trips and ecology lectures — though those exist too.

Flexibility & curriculum

One of the more underrated things about this branch: from semesters 6, 7, and 8, you get significant freedom to choose your own courses based on what you want to specialise in. It's not a rigid pipeline. The most honest way to understand what you'll study is to read the curriculum directly — don't take anyone's word for it, including this guide's.

BTech curriculum — 2024-25 Official PDF ↗

Workload

Honestly? About 1–2 hours a day if you want to actually learn the material. Or an all-nighter before exams if you just want to pass. The branch gives you that choice — which is a double-edged sword.

What seniors go on to do

The range is wider than you'd expect. Research internships at places like Stanford. Consulting firms. ESG roles. Their own startups. Organising Mood Indigo. The diversity of outcomes is one of the more honest signals about what this branch prepares you for — which is less a specific job and more a way of thinking.

Campus life

The department runs its own annual fest called Tatva. Beyond that, students are part of the broader IITB campus life — Mood Indigo, Techfest, and everything else that comes with being at one of the more active campuses in the country.

04

The department

ESED has roots going back to 1977, when environmental engineering started as an interdisciplinary postgraduate programme. It became a formal centre in 1985, and achieved full department status in February 2019 — making it one of the newer departments at IITB.

The undergraduate programme itself has an even shorter history. The BTech-MTech dual degree launched in 2018, but was discontinued in 2023. The standalone BTech programme has only been running since autumn 2023 — the 2026-27 batch will be just the fourth cohort to go through it. That newness is worth knowing: the programme is still finding its shape, which cuts both ways.

On placements

IITB does not release official placement data publicly. More importantly, since the standalone BTech programme has no graduates yet — the first batch completes in 2027 — there is no placement data for this programme at all. Anyone claiming otherwise is guessing.

05

Future scope

"There are a lot of opportunities in research (in India AND abroad) — I had 3 research internship acceptances, 2 from Switzerland and 1 from Stanford. These opportunities are really diverse — from physics, chemistry, math to sustainability, climate, etc. So you can pick any core interest of yours and connect it to environmental science, and there will be good professors in top universities working in it.

As for corporate opportunities (engineering based jobs) — they are limited in India, which is why the placement statistics are not very appealing. But there's a lot of space in startups — you can join or create your own. Lots of chance for that. Even abroad there are loads of startups being founded in this field. There are also relatively more jobs in sustainability, climate, ESG, consulting, analyst areas — but as of now they are not very lucrative. If you find such things exciting, you might have fun.

Overall — you can do a lot of things in this branch if you are sincere and curious. But you most likely will have to pave your own path. Not as many things will be handed to you directly (like great campus internships, placements). But if you are willing to explore on your own, you will have fun.

Disclaimer — Environmental Science and Engineering is an applied branch. If you want to work in engineering related jobs later on, you will need some other expertise. This branch gives you a valuable context and basic knowledge about a certain domain. If you want to go deep in that, you will have to club it with something else (chemical, civil, mechanical, chemistry, etc.). This is a crucial effort you will have to take on your own, as the curriculum is not rigorous enough to sufficiently equip you with these skills right away. So if you are sure you want to do engineering after college, you will have to go an extra mile.

You can always work non-technical jobs in sustainability, climate, and policy — analyst, consultant, policy roles etc. And they are growing as well. But their scene isn't as good right now." — Malhar Ozarde, BTech Environmental Science & Engineering, IIT Bombay · 24b4240@iitb.ac.in

Busting some myths

A few things that get said about this branch during JoSAA season that are worth addressing directly.

Myth

"EnvSci is a lower branch."

A branch is made by the people in it — not by opening and closing ranks. Those ranks reflect a trend, partly because the branch is relatively new and awareness around it is still growing. Judge the curriculum, the faculty, the outcomes — not a number on a list.

Myth

"It's just EVS — pure memorisation."

Some courses do require you to remember things — but that's true of every branch at every IIT. Environmental science at this level is analytical, quantitative, and research-oriented. The EVS course you took in school has almost nothing to do with what's taught here.
